How to Use Queue Decoder

Step-by-step instructions for both modes, field definitions, and tips for reading the queue.

Overview
What Queue Decoder does

When you join a virtual queue on Walmart (or Best Buy, Costco, Sam's Club, and others), your browser silently sends and receives queue data behind the scenes. Queue Decoder lets you intercept that data from your browser's built-in network inspector and translate it into plain English — showing your position, estimated wait time, how fast the queue is moving, and how likely you are to actually get in.

The app works entirely offline. No data ever leaves your browser.

⬡ Decode Single Snapshot

Paste one response to instantly see your queue position, state, estimated turn time, and admission odds. Best for a quick check or when you first join the queue.

⇄ Compare Two Snapshots

Paste an earlier and a later response to calculate how fast the queue is actually moving, how many tickets have been admitted, and project a current turn time. Best for tracking progress over time.

Getting Started — Walmart Queue
Step-by-step: from item page to decoder

Follow these steps after you've added an item to your Walmart cart and landed on the virtual queue waiting room page.

Step 1 — Open DevTools before the queue loads
  1. 1
    Navigate to the Walmart product page. Before clicking anything that triggers the queue, open DevTools by pressing F12 on Windows or Cmd + Option + I on Mac.DevTools must be open before the queue loads so it captures all traffic from the start. Opening it after means you'll miss the early requests.
  2. 2
    Click the Network tab at the top of the DevTools panel.
Step 2 — Filter to validateTickets fetch requests only
  1. 3
    Click the Fetch/XHR button in the filter bar. This hides images, CSS, and other noise.On some Chrome versions this may be labeled "Fetch" or grouped in a dropdown — look to the right of the "All" button.
  2. 4
    In the text filter box at the top, type validate. This filters the list to only validateTickets requests.
  3. 5
    You'll see two types of rows repeating — avoid the preflight rows, use only the fetch rows:❌ Preflight (skip): Empty checkbox icon on the left. Type column says "Preflight". Browser permission checks — no queue data inside.✅ Fetch (use these): Orange/red square icon on the left. Type column says "fetch". Contains your live queue JSON in the Response tab.
Step 3 — Copy the response JSON
  1. 6
    Click one of the orange fetch validateTickets rows to select it.
  2. 7
    In the right panel, click the Response tab (Headers · Payload · Preview · Response · Initiator · Timing).
  3. 8
    You'll see raw JSON starting with [{"site":"usgm".... Click inside it, press Ctrl+A to select all, then Ctrl+C to copy.The response is always a JSON array wrapped in square brackets [ ]. Copy the entire thing including the brackets.
Why validateTickets and not the page URL?
The queue page URL only captures your position at load time and goes stale. The validateTickets endpoint is a live heartbeat — Walmart calls it every 22–37 seconds to refresh your queue data. Each response contains a recalculated estimated turn time, making it far more accurate than the original URL.
Mode 1
⬡ Decode Single Snapshot

Use this mode when you first join the queue, or any time you want a quick read on your current status.

How to use it
  1. 1
    Make sure the Decode Single Snapshot tab is selected (default when you open the app).
  2. 2
    Click Paste to paste from clipboard, or click inside the text box and paste with Ctrl+V.
  3. 3
    Click Decode Queue Data or press Enter. Results appear below.
  4. 4
    To update with a fresh snapshot, simply paste a newer response and decode again.
What the fields mean
FieldWhat it tells you
Queue PositionYour permanent ticket number assigned when you joined. This never changes — it's like a deli counter number, not a "people ahead of you" count.
Queue Statepending = still waiting. valid = you've been admitted, go buy now. inqueue = actively in line.
Refresh IntervalHow often your browser checks in with Walmart's queue server. Typically 22–37 seconds. Each check is one validateTickets request.
Admission OddsWalmart's server-side estimate of whether stock will last long enough to reach you. likely = good chance. unlikely = inventory may run out before your turn.
Time Until Your TurnLive countdown to your estimated turn time from the snapshot. Does not account for actual queue velocity — use Compare mode for a real reading.
Next Heartbeat barCountdown to the next validateTickets refresh. When it reaches zero, Walmart has sent a new position update — grab a fresh Response for updated data.
Important: The countdown timer in Single Snapshot mode is based only on the snapshot you pasted. A frozen queue will still show the timer counting down even though your real wait isn't changing. Use Compare Two Snapshots to see actual queue velocity.
Admission Odds badge colors
likely unlikely medium unknown

If unlikely persists across multiple refreshes and queue velocity is near 0.00x, inventory has most likely sold through and the queue is in a holding pattern waiting for a restock.

Mode 2
⇄ Compare Two Snapshots

This mode answers the real question: is the queue actually moving? It takes two responses grabbed at different times and calculates queue velocity, tickets admitted, and a projected current turn time.

Why this is more accurate than Single Snapshot: Walmart constantly recalculates your expectedTurnTimeUnixTimestamp — it can go up (backlog) or down (fast admissions) regardless of your actual position. The only reliable signal is comparing the remaining wait at two different real-world capture times. Compare mode does this math automatically.
How to use it
  1. 1
    Click the ⇄ Compare Two Snapshots tab.
  2. 2
    In DevTools, click the earliest validateTickets fetch row (scroll to the top). Copy its Response JSON.
  3. 3
    Click Paste → A. The preview line below confirms it parsed correctly.You can also paste manually. The preview updates automatically as you type.
  4. 4
    Go back to DevTools and click the most recent validateTickets fetch row (scroll to the bottom). Copy its Response JSON.
  5. 5
    Click Paste → B.
  6. 6
    Click Analyze Velocity. Results appear below.
Tip — spacing your snapshots: The further apart your two snapshots are in real time, the more accurate the velocity reading. Aim for at least 10–15 minutes between them. If you have an hour of DevTools history, use the very first and very last rows.
What the compare fields mean
FieldWhat it tells you
Wait DroppedHow much your remaining wait actually shrank between snapshots. A red + prefix means your wait increased (queue backlogged).
Real Time ElapsedActual wall-clock time between when you grabbed Snapshot A and B. Derived from the payload's internal timestamps.
Queue VelocityRatio of wait burned vs real time. 1.0x = real-time pace. 2.0x = twice as fast (good). 0.06x = nearly frozen (bad). 0.00x = inventory gone.
Est. Tickets AheadRough estimate of how many people are ahead of you right now, based on remaining wait at ~20 seconds per admitted customer.
Projected Turn TimeYour estimated turn time extrapolated to the current moment using queue velocity. More accurate than the single snapshot countdown.
Admitted Between SnapsEstimated tickets admitted during the time between your two snapshots.
Velocity color guide
1.5x+ Fast 0.5–1.5x Normal Under 0.5x Slow

Velocity below 0.10x combined with unlikely admission odds almost always means inventory has sold through and the queue is frozen waiting for a restock.

Reading the Queue
What the signals actually mean
You're likely to get in
Admission odds flip to likely · Velocity above 1.0x · Projected turn time under 10 minutes · State still pending but dropping fast
Queue is frozen — inventory sold out
Velocity is 0.00x · Admission odds stuck on unlikely · Wait Dropped shows 0m 0s across multiple compares · Projected turn time barely moves

Walmart does not close the queue when stock sells out. Instead it pushes your expectedTurnTimeUnixTimestamp forward in near-perfect lockstep with real time, creating a frozen estimate that never counts down. This is the holding pattern — waiting for a restock.

It's your turn — act fast
state flips from pending → valid · Walmart shows a "Buy Now" button · You have a short window (a few minutes) to complete checkout before your slot expires
Queue backlogged — wait increased
Wait Dropped shows a red +X minutes · Walmart pushed your turn time out due to a surge of new entrants or inventory recalculation. Typically stabilizes after a restock wave passes.
Tips
Getting the most out of it
Order doesn't matter in Compare mode

You can paste Snapshot A and B in the wrong order — the app detects the order automatically using the internal capture timestamps and swaps them if needed.

Keep DevTools open the whole time

DevTools only logs requests made while it is open. If you close and reopen it, the earlier history is lost. Keep it open in a separate window to maintain a full history of all validateTickets calls.

Use the earliest row available for Snapshot A

The longer the time gap between your two snapshots, the more accurate the velocity calculation. If you've been in the queue for an hour, the very first validateTickets row gives you the longest possible comparison window.

Show raw JSON for troubleshooting

If a field shows N/A or the decode fails, click Show raw JSON at the bottom of the results to see exactly what fields were returned.

The app works for other retailers too

Queue Decoder also supports Best Buy, Costco, Sam's Club, Queue-it, Cloudflare Waiting Room, and Ticketmaster. The process is identical — filter DevTools for the queue-related fetch request, copy the Response JSON, and paste it in.

⚠️ Work in Progress
Queue Decoder is still being refined and may have some quirks depending on the site or payload format. It works well for most Walmart drops but your mileage may vary on other retailers. When in doubt, use the Show Raw JSON toggle to verify what data was actually parsed.
